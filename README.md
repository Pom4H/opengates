# Open Gates

## When a claim becomes an accepted fact

I created Open Gates as a way to look at business digitization from a slightly
different angle.

Many operational problems do not begin with the absence of a large system. They
begin with a fact that has been claimed, but not yet accepted.

Someone says the work is done.

Someone says the goods were delivered.

Someone says the volume was completed.

Someone says the batch passed quality control.

Someone says the service was provided.

But until another person, role, or side accepts that statement, the operation
remains suspended.

Money cannot be paid with confidence.

The next stage cannot be opened safely.

Responsibility is unclear.

Risk hangs in the air.

Open Gates is my proposal to make those moments visible as separate acceptance
nodes.

## The acceptance node

An acceptance node answers a small set of practical questions:

- Who made the claim?
- What exactly was claimed?
- What evidence supports it?
- What should it be checked against?
- Who has the authority to accept or reject it?
- What decision was made?
- What can happen after the decision?

The idea is not to replace every system a company already uses.

Most companies already have accounting, warehouse tools, documents, estimates,
payments, spreadsheets, calendars, and internal systems. Open Gates is meant to
sit between them: the place where a claim becomes an accepted fact.

## A registry of gates

To make this useful, a company needs a registry of acceptance nodes.

For example:

- in construction, acceptance of completed work;
- in logistics, confirmation of delivery;
- in a warehouse, a mismatch with an invoice or delivery note;
- in manufacturing, acceptance of a batch after quality control;
- in healthcare and insurance, confirmation that a service was provided.

For each node, the company can describe the rules: what data is required, what
evidence is valid, who can decide, what stays blocked before acceptance, and what
is triggered after acceptance.

From there, the node can be connected to existing systems.

A claim may come from one system. Documents and evidence may come from another.
A person with the right role makes the decision. The accepted fact returns to
the places where it matters: payment, the next stage, reporting, or the
operational map.

## The role of assistants

Assistants such as Claude or Codex are especially interesting in this model.

They do not have to be just chat interfaces. Connected to tools, they can help
find a disputed case, collect the relevant data, compare a claim with evidence,
show the mismatch, and prepare the decision for a human reviewer.

But responsibility stays with the person.

The assistant does not make the business decision. It helps people reach the
point where they can make that decision consciously and faster.

## What Open Gates is

For me, Open Gates is not a finished product.

It is a proposal: an attempt to describe a new layer for business operations.

That layer is built around one question:

**When does a claim become an accepted fact?**

The answer matters because money, deadlines, risk, and real control over an
operation often depend on that exact moment.
